## Writing about XAI while avoiding the ÔÇťexplanation equals causeÔÇŁ trap

In ML+XAI literature reviewsÔÇöespecially those aiming to connect explanations to domain/physics/process causesÔÇöthe most common conceptual mistake is to **slide from association to causation**. You can make mechanism-oriented writing more defensible by using a **three-layer claim structure** in the literature review:

- **Layer 1 (model-behaviour claim):** what the model relied on _within the dataset and modelling pipeline_ (an association claim). ţłÇciteţłéturn1search1ţłéturn1search0ţłéturn2search0ţłü
- **Layer 2 (domain-plausibility claim):** whether that reliance is consistent with known constraints or process knowledge (a plausibility check; not yet causality). ţłÇciteţłéturn3search11ţłéturn3search6ţłü
- **Layer 3 (mechanism/causal claim):** whether there is evidence or assumptions sufficient to claim causality (requires causal assumptions, interventions, or mechanistic modelling). ţłÇciteţłéturn2search3ţłéturn2search31ţłéturn2search27ţłü

When your goal is explicitly to connect ML predictions and XAI outputs to physics/process causes, you will often cite and integrate literature on **theory-guided or physics-informed modelling**, where domain knowledge is incorporated into learning and can make explanations more meaningfully aligned with physical constraints. ţłÇciteţłéturn3search11ţłéturn3search6ţłéturn3search19ţłü Physics-informed neural networks, for example, are framed as neural networks trained while respecting governing physical laws, and theory-guided data science is framed as integrating scientific knowledge with data-driven modelling to support scientific discovery and interpretable models in scientific domains. ţłÇciteţłéturn3search6ţłéturn3search11ţłéturn3search19ţłü
