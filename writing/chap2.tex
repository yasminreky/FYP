\section{2.1 Machine Learning in Solar Power Generation Forecasting}

The increasing reliance on solar power generation has led to an increasing use of machine learning (ML) for improving forecasting accuracy. As noted in Zhang et al. (2025), the application of XGBoost for solar power forecasting has proven to be a highly effective method due to its ability to handle large, noisy datasets and model the complex, non-linear relationships between weather variables and solar output. Similarly, LSTM networks have demonstrated an ability to capture long-term temporal dependencies in solar irradiance and temperature data, outperforming more traditional regression methods like SVR (Lv et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025).

However, solar forecasting is not without its challenges. One of the key issues with machine learning models is their inability to easily explain the decision-making process, which is critical when solar forecasting is integrated into grid systems. XAI (Explainable Artificial Intelligence) methods like SHAP have emerged as tools to address this challenge, making models like LSTM and XGBoost more transparent. As demonstrated by Zhang et al. (2025), SHAP helps explain the impact of features such as solar irradiance and temperature on predictions, enabling more informed decision-making in energy management.

Furthermore, the need for explainability in forecasting models has led to the integration of Explainable AI (XAI) techniques, such as SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations). Recent work by Bakht et al. (2024) demonstrated that SHAP could provide insights into the feature importance of variables like temperature and cloud cover, thus enhancing the interpretability of complex models like LSTM and XGBoost. These advancements in XAI are critical in operational settings, where stakeholders must understand how meteorological variables influence power generation predictions (Eren et al., 2025; Lv et al., 2025).

\section{2.2 Overview of Machine Learning Algorithms for Solar Power Generation Forecasting}

This section outlines the primary machine learning algorithms used in solar power generation forecasting, including Support Vector Machines (SVM), Random Forest (RF), Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. Each method is evaluated for its strengths, weaknesses, and suitability for various forecasting tasks, particularly in the context of solar power generation using weather sensor data.

\subsection{2.2.1 Support Vector Regression (SVR)

Support Vector Regression (SVR) is widely applied in solar power forecasting due to its capability to model complex, non-linear relationships between weather variables and solar generation. In Zhao et al. (2025), SVR was found to be effective in short-term solar forecasting, particularly in situations where data is sparse and feature interactions are not complex. However, SVR has limitations in long-term forecasting due to its inability to model temporal dependencies, which are essential for accurate predictions over longer horizons (Lv et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025).

Recent research has shown that hybrid models, such as SVR-LSTM, can overcome these limitations by combining SVR’s ability to handle non-linearity with LSTM's capacity to model sequential data, thus improving long-term forecasting accuracy (Zeng et al., 2025). This hybrid approach was demonstrated by Lv et al. (2025) to reduce the mean absolute error (MAE) by 18% compared to SVR alone.

XGBoost, in particular, has been highlighted as a highly effective model for solar forecasting due to its robustness in feature selection and its ability to handle missing data (Lv et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). As Yang et al. (2025) noted, XGBoost achieved significant improvements in predictive performance, with an R² value of 0.94, outperforming traditional models like SVR.

Despite their strong performance, RF and XGBoost do not account for temporal dependencies in solar irradiance data, which can limit their effectiveness for long-term forecasting tasks. Hybrid models combining XGBoost with LSTM have been suggested to address this issue by leveraging LSTM's ability to handle sequential data and XGBoost's ability to manage complex feature interactions (Lv et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025).

\subsection{2.2.3 Gradient Boosting Models: XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost}

Gradient Boosting Machines (GBMs), such as XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost, are increasingly being used for solar power forecasting due to their computational efficiency and ability to model complex, high-dimensional data. XGBoost has been shown to outperform many other ML models in solar energy forecasting, particularly when handling weather data from multiple sensors (Lv et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025). Zhang et al. (2025) demonstrated that XGBoost provided more accurate predictions than traditional models, with an improvement in RMSE of 22% when tested on solar irradiance data.

However, like RF, XGBoost and other gradient boosting methods are not inherently designed to capture temporal relationships in the data, which are critical for accurate long-term forecasting. To address this, XGBoost is often combined with LSTM or other sequential models. Zeng et al. (2025) showed that this hybrid model improved forecasting accuracy, reducing MAE by 16% compared to the standalone XGBoost model.

\subsection{2.2.4 Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Networks}

LSTM networks are among the most popular deep learning models for solar power forecasting due to their ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies in sequential data. In a study by Lv et al. (2025), LSTM was found to outperform traditional models like SVR and RF, particularly in forecasting solar energy generation over longer periods. Zhang et al. (2025) also demonstrated that LSTM provided a 24% improvement in accuracy for solar power prediction when compared to simpler models, especially in environments with high seasonal and diurnal fluctuations.

One of the challenges with LSTM models, however, is their black-box nature, which makes it difficult to interpret the predictions. This has led to the integration of XAI techniques such as SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) to provide insights into how specific weather features, such as solar irradiance and temperature, influence the model’s predictions (Zhao et al., 2025; Lv et al., 2025). Eren et al. (2025) used SHAP to show how solar irradiance is the most significant factor in LSTM predictions, highlighting the importance of explainability in solar forecasting systems.

\section{2.3 Feature Engineering in Solar Power Forecasting}

\subsection{2.3.1 Feature Characteristics}

Effective feature engineering is vital in improving the performance of machine learning models for solar power forecasting. Raw weather sensor data, such as solar irradiance, temperature, and humidity, must often be transformed into features that can better capture the underlying temporal and spatial patterns driving solar power generation. Zhao et al. (2025) highlighted the importance of including lagged variables and rolling averages to capture daily and seasonal cycles in solar irradiance, thus improving the performance of models like XGBoost and LSTM.

Additionally, incorporating domain-specific features, such as the solar panel tilt angle and cloud cover index, has been shown to significantly improve forecasting accuracy by accounting for physical characteristics that affect energy generation (Lv et al., 2025). Yang et al. (2025) demonstrated that the inclusion of these features improved LSTM forecasting accuracy by 10% compared to models using only standard weather variables. Furthermore, feature selection, such as identifying the most influential variables using SHAP, can guide model refinement and improve predictive performance (Eren et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025).

\section{2.4 Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) in Solar Power Forecasting}
The increasing complexity of machine learning models, especially deep learning models like LSTM and XGBoost, necessitates the use of Explainable AI (XAI) methods. XAI helps provide transparency, making it easier for stakeholders to trust and understand model predictions. In solar power forecasting, SHAP and LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) are commonly used to explain the influence of different weather features on model outputs. Zhang et al. (2025) demonstrated that SHAP could explain how variables like solar irradiance and temperature drive the predictions of LSTM models, thus improving model interpretability and trustworthiness.

\subsection{2.5 Dataset Diversity and Model Generalizability}
A major challenge in solar forecasting is ensuring that models generalize well across diverse datasets and environmental conditions. Solar power generation depends on local weather conditions, and datasets often vary based on geographical location, time of year, and sensor configuration. Yang et al. (2025) found that LSTM models trained on data from one region performed well when tested on data from similar locations but struggled when applied to regions with different weather conditions.
